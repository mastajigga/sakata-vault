# Complete Article: Iluo, the Shared Breath of the Doubles

*Nkundi ya bakoko: "Nkasa moko ekoki kokanga mbuma te."*
— Proverb of our ancestors: A single leaf cannot wrap a fruit. Likewise, a single being cannot contain all the light of the world; sometimes, the spirits choose to divide it into two bodies from the mother's womb.

## 1. The Call of the Twin Rivers
Come closer to the fire, my child, and listen to the song of the forest. Do you hear that murmur that seems to come from two directions at once? It is the secret of the *Iluo* (the twins). Here, in the green land of Mai-Ndombe, we say that he who walks alone is never truly alone, for his shadow always precedes him. But for the Iluo, the shadow is not made of darkness: it is made of flesh, blood, and an identical breath.

The elders of the village of Mposo, under the benevolent eye of the great chief Augustin Nienie — a man whose wisdom guided our steps through the mists of history — knew that the birth of two beings from the same womb was not a mere chance of the flesh. It is a liquid blessing, for the Iluo come from the Kisakata River itself. It is said that a mother whose womb remained barren like burnt earth went to cry by the water's edge. Two sacred fish, guides sent by those who preceded us, jumped out of the foam. Upon her return, she carried within her not one, but two promises of life.

## 2. The Mystery of the United Matrix
Imagine a wild fruit that splits in two to reveal two perfect seeds. Such is the mystery of the Iluo. In our culture, twinning is the sign of a fertility that overflows, a generosity of the earth that decides to give more than what the simple body can hope for. But watch out, my son, for what is twice as strong is also twice as fragile.

The Iluo is not an anomaly, it is a message. Every time twins are born into a family, it is as if the ancestors are knocking at the door to remind us that the balance of the world rests on duality. Day and night, rain and sun, the visible and the invisible. The Iluo is the living bridge between these two worlds. He carries within him such a spiritual charge that the entire village must prepare to welcome him with respect and awe.

## 3. The Legacy of the Bazia: The Intelligence of the Double
Remember the words recorded in the precious book *Chef et apôtre*. It speaks of the Bazia tribe, that intelligent and proud people whose roots we share across the Lukenie. Chief Nienie, whom the white men called Augustin, saw in his subjects an exceptional mental strength. Why? Because among the Bazia, we have always known how to cultivate "the intelligence of the double."

To know that one has a double is to learn very early that one's own thought is not the only one in the world. The Iluo learns negotiation even before knowing how to speak. He understands sharing before knowing possession. It is this ancestral wisdom that allowed our chiefs to dialogue with foreign travelers without losing their soul. The Iluo is the school of diplomacy in the blood.

## 4. The Strength of the Elder and the Guard of the Junior
Everything that is two has a hierarchy in the invisible. Among the Iluo, the first who comes out is not necessarily "the old one" in the chronological sense, but he is the bearer of the torch. It is often said that one is the Warrior and the other is the Sage. One carries the spear to protect the hearth, the other carries the word to appease the gods.

Yet, do not believe them divided. They are like the two hands of a single man: one can hold the bow, but it needs the other to stretch the string. In the tales of our grandparents, it is said that never was an Iluo defeated in battle as long as his brother breathed by his side. Their hearts beat in unison, even if leagues of forest separate them. If one stumbles, the other feels the earth tremble beneath his feet.

## 5. The Rite of Naming: Kofi and Kofi, Nimi and Nimi
Listen well, for the name is the key to the soul. When the Iluo arrive, they are not given names at random. We use complementary names, mirrors of sounds. Sometimes, they are called *Kofi* and *Kofi* (the Strength that repeats), or *Nimi* and *Nimi* (the Water that flows in two rivers).

Repeating the same name is not a lack of imagination, my child. It is a protection. By calling both with the same breath, we bind their destinies so that evil spirits cannot separate them. If sickness seeks "Kofi," it will find two forces instead of one, and it will flee into the thickets, struck down by this shared resistance.

## 6. Sacred Architecture: The Double Hut and the Hearth of the Soul
When a Sakata mother brings Iluo into the world, time stops at the village. The father does not sit with arms crossed contemplating the rain; he must become the architect of balance. He immediately builds a hut of a special kind, which the elders call the dwelling of the shared breath. It is a house with two distinct entrances, for if the souls are sisters, the bodies need space to breathe. A single hearth burns yet in the center, for the heat that animates them is the same.

Imagine the precision of this gesture: every red wood branch, every braided palm leaf is placed with a prayer. It is in this dwelling that the protection rites are performed, away from prying eyes. Wild honey is poured there, stolen from the bees of the high forest, so that their temperament is as sweet as the nectar. Palm nuts are burned there, their thick smoke rising toward the ancestors, carrying the fragrance of this lineage that doubles itself. Every gesture counts, for the balance of the Iluo is also the balance of the weather. A disgruntled twin is like a storm gathering over the calm waters of the Lukenie. If the twins cry without reason, the mokambi looks at the sky and knows that the drought is not far off.

## 7. The Taboo of Union: The Dance of the Currents
Here is a wisdom that the youth of today sometimes forgets in the bustle of the cities: the Iluo cannot unite with another Iluo from a foreign family without a liberation rite that only the very old know. Why this severity? Because the meeting of two pairs of twins is like the meeting of four flooding rivers. It would create a spiritual whirlpool so powerful that even the sturdiest pirogue would be broken.

Thus, we avoid tangling the threads of destiny. We believe that the spirits need clarity. The elders of the Bazia tribe, those men whose intelligence is praised by Jules Denis in his chronicles, knew that the mixing of two twinning lineages would risk making the children born of these unions too "electric," too close to the spirit world, making them unfit to walk on firm ground. We respect these taboos not out of superstition, but through a deep understanding of the energies that inhabit us.

## 8. The Messengers of the Water: Dialogue Beneath the Surface
An Iluo does not look at the river like you and me, my child. For him, the Lukenie is an open book. It is whispered that the twins have an ear that always remains under the water, even when they are plowing the fields. They feel the vibration of fish schools, they hear the snapping of crocodile tails in distant marshes.

This connection with the liquid element comes from their very birth. They are the envoys of the strength of the water. In moments of great crisis, when food is scarce, we ask the Iluo to sit on the bank and "speak" to their aquatic brothers. Often, in the days that follow, the fishermen's nets come back full to bursting, as if the river had recognized the voice of its children. The famous researcher Jan Vansina called this cosmic duality, but for us, it is simply the respect of a family that extends beneath the waters.

## 9. Chief Nienie and the Protection of the Breath
The great Augustin Nienie, chief of the Bazia, carried this respect within him. Although he embraced the prayer of the missionaries, he never denied the strength of the roots. He knew that to lead 5000 intelligent souls, one had to understand what bound them to the invisible. He protected the twins of the village like crown jewels.

It is said that in Mposo, under his reign, no Iluo ever lacked for anything. Chief Augustin knew that if an injustice struck a twin, the entire intelligence of the tribe would suffer. He acted as the junior protects the senior in the sacred tale. It is this legacy of care and vigilance that we must carry today toward the digital future.

## 10. Modernity and Tradition: Light on the Screen
Today, my child, our words travel by the invisible threads of the air, on these screens that shine at night. But do not be fooled by novelty. An Iluo typing on a keyboard remains an Iluo. This duality, this capacity to see the light and the shadow at the same time, is what makes us Sakata capable of living in the 21st century while keeping the scent of the forest in our hair.

Today's twins use their "double sight" to heal wounded hearts or to build bridges between peoples. They are our mediators, our interpreters between the past of stone and the future of glass. Respect the twin you meet, for his soul is a two-story library, and every step he climbs brings us closer to the supreme wisdom.

## 11. Conclusion: The Great Circle of Breath
My son, the fire sinks and the forest spirits begin to awaken. Remember this: the Iluo is not a mirror, he is an eternal traveling companion. For the Sakata people, twinning is the proof that we were not cast alone upon this earth. We were born with an alliance, a pact of blood and sky.

May we all, even if we were born alone, learn the lesson of the Iluo: to know that our strength resides in the one who walks by our side. And when you cross the river to join the ancestors, hope that you will find your double waiting for you on the other shore to show you the way.
